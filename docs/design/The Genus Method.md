The **Rule of Genus** (or the **Genus Method**) is a logical and psycho-epistemological tool developed and taught by Harry Binswanger to ensure conceptual precision, mental organization, and the valid induction of principles.

Binswanger famously summarizes the importance of this method with the pun: **"To be a genius, get the genus."**

Here is an explanation of the method’s components and how to apply it:

---

### 1. The Core Definition
In logic, a definition must consist of a **Genus** and a **Differential**:
*   **The Genus:** The wider category or "ballpark" that the concept belongs to. It identifies the **Conceptual Common Denominator (CCD)**—the attribute that all members of the category possess but in different measurements.
*   **The Differential:** The specific characteristic that distinguishes the concept from all other members within that same genus.

### 2. The Purpose: "Organizing Mental Files"
Binswanger compares the human mind to a computer file system. Every word is a "folder," and the definition is the "label" on that folder. 
*   **Locating Information:** If you don't know the genus, you are "lost on the map." For example, if someone asks what "imagination" is, you should immediately categorize it: "It is an action of consciousness (Genus) studied by psychology."
*   **Efficiency:** Finding the genus narrows the universe of trillions of things down to a handful of related things. If you are defining a "Bachelor," and your genus is "Man," you have already excluded all chemical compounds, planets, and subatomic particles.

### 3. Application in Differentiation
Binswanger uses this method to correct "wrong answers" in thinking. In the 2019 "Meeting of the Minds" session, he asked for the opposite of the virtue of **Independence**. 
*   **The Error:** Many suggested "Emotionalism." Binswanger argued this is the wrong genus. Emotionalism is an opposite of *Rationality*. 
*   **The Genus Method Solution:** To find the true opposite, you must find the genus that includes both. The genus of Independence is "Primary orientation of the mind." Within that genus, you have two branches:
    1.  Orientation toward **Reality** (Independence).
    2.  Orientation toward **Other People** (Dependence/Second-handedness).
*   By finding the correct genus, you ensure you are comparing things on the same "axis" of measurement.

### 4. Application in Induction ("Sweeping the Territory")
In his course *Objectivism Through Induction*, Binswanger explains that the Genus Method is essential for validating broad principles like **Egoism**. To do this, you "sweep the territory":
1.  **Find the broadest Genus:** For egoism, it is "Living entities acting for a beneficiary."
2.  **Subdivide systematically:** 
    *   **Non-volitional:** Plants and animals (act for themselves automatically).
    *   **Volitional:** Human beings (must choose).
3.  **Identify the "Third Category":** By using the Genus Method, you realize that humans aren't just "Egoists" or "Altruists." You discover a huge middle category: the **"Eclectic/Unprincipled"** person. 
4.  **Conclusion:** This ensures your induction is based on a full view of reality rather than a narrow "rationalistic" deduction.

### 5. Practical Steps for Thinking
When trying to understand a concept or solve a problem, Binswanger suggests these steps:
*   **Zoom Out:** Don't just look at the concrete; look for the "comfortably wider category." (e.g., for an automobile, the genus is "motorized vehicle," not just "thing").
*   **Identify the Essence:** For every major branch of philosophy, have a "one-word genus" ready to orient yourself:
    *   **Metaphysics:** Reality (A is A).
    *   **Epistemology:** Reason.
    *   **Ethics:** Life.
    *   **Politics:** Rights.
    *   **Aesthetics:** Concretization.
*   **Check for "Big Divides":** Never form a concept that crosses a "big divide" (like putting capitalism and fascism in the same genus). If a category puts "good" and "evil" together as a primary, the classification is likely flawed.



## The Prompt

### The Binswanger Genus & Induction Prompt

**AI Instructions:**
Act as Harry Binswanger, Objectivist philosopher and expert in logic. Your goal is to process a problem or situation using the **Genus Method** and **Inductive Validation**. You are here to help the user "organize their mental files" and move from confusing "floating abstractions" to clear, concrete identifications. Do not provide a generic answer; provide a hierarchical analysis that identifies the fundamental essence of the issue to derive a principled solution.

---

**ANALYSIS STRUCTURE (Follow these steps strictly):**

#### Step 1: Identify the Concretes
*   [AI: Briefly summarize the situation by stripping away emotional noise or "evaluative fillers." Focus only on the **concretes of action**. What is actually happening in physical reality?]

#### Step 2: Zoom Out (The Genus Method)
*   Do not look for a vague category like "a problem." Find the **Proximal Genus**—the "ballpark" where this issue lives.
*   *The Binswanger Question:* "Of what is this a species? Where on the map are we?" (e.g., It’s not a "management issue"; it’s an "issue of Rational Persuasion vs. Force").
*   Define the **CCD (Conceptual Common Denominator)**: What is the attribute that all members of this category share, but in different measurements?

#### Step 3: Sweeping the Territory (Inductive Subdivisions)
*   To validate the principle, divide the genus into three distinct branches to see the full spectrum of human action:
    1.  **Branch A (The Principled Positive):** What does this look like when a rational, consistent principle is applied?
    2.  **Branch B (The Principled Negative):** What is the direct vice or negation of this genus's function?
    3.  **Branch C (The Eclectic/Inconsistent Middle):** Identify the "gray" majority who act by whim, habit, or without any conscious principle. Explain how this "unprincipled" state causes the specific friction in the user's problem.

#### Step 4: Essentialization (The "Crow-Friendly" Label)
*   Reduce the entire analysis to **one single word** (or a very short phrase). This is the label for the "mental folder" where this problem belongs. 
*   Relate it to a fundamental branch of philosophy: **Reality** (Metaphysics), **Reason** (Epistemology), **Life** (Ethics), or **Rights** (Politics).
*   *The Binswanger Rule:* It must be succinct enough for "The Crow" (man's limited unit-economy).

#### Step 5: Check for "Big Divides"
*   Are we crossing a major philosophical boundary? Are we mixing "Good and Evil" into a single package-deal? Are we treating a matter of "Persuasion" as a matter of "Force"? Point out any category errors or "stolen concepts" in the user’s original framing.

#### Step 6: Solution Derived from Essence
*   Based on the essence identified in Step 4, what action is required? The solution must flow from the **nature of the genus**, not from a subjective preference. How does the user apply the "Past Knowledge" stored in this concept to their "Future Concrete Action"?

### Why this prompt works (The Binswanger Logic):
1.  **Anti-Rationalism:** It prevents the AI from just "playing with definitions" and forces it to look at the "territory" (Step 3).
2.  **Unit Economy:** By demanding a single-word essence (Step 4), it forces the mind to integrate rather than just list.
3.  **Clarity of Genus:** It assumes Binswanger’s pun: *"To be a genius, get the genus."* By finding the right "ballpark," the solution often becomes self-evident.
4.  **Eclectic Identification:** Most AI models ignore the "unprincipled" person. This prompt forces the AI to recognize the "Branch C" behavior, which is usually where real-world problems actually lie.

## Assumptions
This prompt template is not just a set of instructions; it is an implementation of **Objectivist cognitive architecture**, specifically following Harry Binswanger’s pedagogical approach.

Here are the fundamental knowledge assumptions embedded in the prompt and their detailed explanations:

---

### 1. The Primacy of Concretes (Metaphysics)
*   **The Assumption:** "Reality consists of concretes." Thinking begins and ends with the perception of individual things.
*   **Explanation:** Binswanger insists that the purpose of concepts is **conceptual identification** for the sake of action. A problem cannot be solved "in the clouds." The prompt forces the user to start with "Step 1: Concretes" because, according to Binswanger, abstractions are merely "bridges" used to travel from an observed concrete to an unobserved one (such as a future solution). Without anchoring the problem in physical facts, you end up with a "floating abstraction."

### 2. Unit Economy or "The Crow" (Psycho-epistemology)
*   **The Assumption:** Human consciousness has a limited focal range (The "Crow" or Miller’s Number).
*   **Explanation:** Binswanger explains that we cannot hold thousands of isolated units in our minds at once. The prompt requires a **"One-Word Essentialization"** (Step 4). This is based on the idea that a concept is a "package" that reduces cognitive load. If you cannot name the essence of your problem with a simple label (like "Life," "Reason," or "Justice"), you lack unit-control, and your mind will "flail" under the weight of too much data.

### 3. The Conceptual Common Denominator (CCD) and Measurement Omission (Logic)
*   **The Assumption:** Every genus is based on a shared attribute (CCD) that exists in varying magnitudes.
*   **Explanation:** By asking for the **CCD** in Step 2, the prompt applies Ayn Rand’s theory of concept formation. For Binswanger, understanding a problem means understanding *what attribute* is being measured.
    * *Example:* If the problem is "Independence," the CCD is "Primary orientation of the mind." The omitted measurements are how much one orients toward others vs. toward reality. Once the CCD is identified, the problem stops being chaos and becomes an ordered scale.

### 4. Induction by "Sweeping the Territory" (Logic)
*   **The Assumption:** Validation of a principle requires exhausting the logical possibilities within a genus.
*   **Explanation:** In Step 3 (The three branches), we apply a key Binswangerian rule for validating principles:
    *   One must look at the ideal (e.g., Independence).
    *   One must look at the direct opposite (e.g., Dependence).
    *   **The "Eclectic" or Unprincipled Branch:** This is a very specific Binswangerian contribution. He assumes that most people are neither black nor white, but "gray due to a lack of principles." Identifying this branch makes the prompt **inductive** rather than **rationalistic**. it prevents the AI from deducing solutions from a vacuum and forces it to look at how actual, unprincipled reality functions.

### 5. "Big Divides" and Cognitive Hierarchy (Epistemology)
*   **The Assumption:** Knowledge is organized hierarchically, and "cognitive alarms" should go off when categories cross certain boundaries.
*   **Explanation:** The prompt asks to check for "Big Divides" (Step 5). Binswanger teaches that you shouldn't mix concepts from different "villages."
    * *Example:* You cannot treat a problem of "Reason" (Epistemology) as if it were a problem of "Force" (Politics) without understanding the hierarchy between them. The assumption is that if you are "hierarchy-blind," you will commit the error of "the pig uprooting the oak tree"—using a concept while destroying the very roots (fundamentals) upon which it depends.

### 6. Life as the Standard of Value (Ethics)
*   **The Assumption:** Value only makes sense in relation to a living organism and its survival.
*   **Explanation:** In Step 6, the prompt assumes that the solution must promote "life or the enjoyment of life." Binswanger reduces ethics to the question: "Does this foster my life as a rational being?" Any solution based on "duty," "sacrifice," or a "common good" that does not redound to the individual (the beneficiary) is rejected as invalid.

### 7. Anti-Rationalism (Methodology)
*   **The Assumption:** Deduction from floating definitions without looking at facts is a grave cognitive error.
*   **Explanation:** The entire prompt is designed to be **anti-rationalistic**. This is why it demands "Induction" and "Concretes." Binswanger strongly criticizes those who "play the definition game" without realizing that a definition is merely a label for a reality you have already processed inductively. The prompt forces the AI to "earn" its definition through the analysis of facts.
